MAS Monetary Policy Statement at a Glance

What did MAS decide?

MAS is letting the Singapore dollar strengthen a bit faster

In simple terms, MAS wants the Singapore dollar to rise a little faster over time. It did not change the width of the policy band or move the whole band up or down.
What is happening in the economy?

Energy prices are up, while growth is still solid but losing some speed

Prices coming in from abroad

Shipping disruptions in the Middle East have pushed up the prices of oil, gas, and related inputs. That means Singapore is paying more for some imports, which can eventually feed into household bills and business costs.

Activity at home

Singapore’s economy still grew strongly in the first quarter of 2026, helped by manufacturing and services linked to global tech investment. But growth dipped slightly compared with the previous quarter, which suggests the pace is starting to cool.

How does MAS see the outlook?

MAS expects slower growth this year and somewhat higher inflation

Inflation in 2026

1.5–2.5%
MAS now expects both core inflation and overall inflation to come in within this higher range.

GDP growth in 2026

2.0–4.0%
MAS said growth this year should be slower than in 2025, and it will update the full-year forecast separately.
What are the risks?

The main risks are a longer energy shock and a sharper slowdown in global demand

Energy prices stay high

If energy supplies stay tight for longer, inflation could climb further and growth could weaken more.

Factories face shortages

If important inputs become harder to get, production could slow suddenly in Singapore and abroad.

Global financial conditions tighten

If borrowing becomes more expensive around the world, businesses and consumers may pull back.

Tech investment cools

If global spending on AI and electronics slows unexpectedly, one of Singapore’s current growth supports could weaken.
